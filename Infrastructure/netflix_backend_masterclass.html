From Bare Metal to Netflix: The Complete Backend Infrastructure Story

This guide starts from a single server sitting in a rack and builds—concept by concept—until you understand how Netflix serves 260+ million users simultaneously without a single second of downtime. Every concept links to the next like chapters in a novel. No prerequisites needed. If you can read this sentence, you're ready.

1. The Big Picture: What Happens When You Press Play

Before we dive into individual technologies, let's paint the complete picture. When you open the Netflix app and press play on "Stranger Things", here is what happens in the next 200 milliseconds:

The 200-Millisecond Journey
  1. Your device sends a request over the internet: "I want to watch Stranger Things, Episode 1."
  2. The request hits a DNS server that translates netflix.com to an IP address.
  3. An Application Load Balancer receives the request and picks the healthiest backend server to handle it.
  4. The Authentication Service checks: "Is this person a paying subscriber? Are they allowed to watch this in their country?"
  5. The Recommendation Engine already decided hours ago which thumbnail to show you (yes, different users see different thumbnails for the same show).
  6. The Streaming Service figures out the best video quality for your internet speed and picks the closest CDN server (Open Connect Appliance) to your house—maybe one sitting inside your ISP's office building.
  7. Video data starts streaming in small chunks from that nearby server to your screen.

Every single term above—DNS, Load Balancer, Authentication, CDN, Microservices—will be explained from scratch in the sections that follow. By the end, you'll understand each piece and how they snap together like LEGO bricks to build a machine that handles 400+ million hours of video per day.

2. Data Centers & Top-of-Rack (ToR) Switches

Everything starts with a building. Not a fancy glass office—a data center: a massive, climate-controlled warehouse filled with thousands of servers stacked in metal shelves called racks.

Beginner Analogy: The Library Imagine a gigantic library. Each bookshelf is a Server Rack—a tall metal frame that holds 20-42 servers stacked vertically, like books on a shelf. The entire library building is the Data Center. And just like a library needs a catalog system so you can find the right book, a data center needs networking equipment so servers can find and talk to each other.
Deep Dive: The Internal Physics

What is a Server Rack?

A server rack is a standardised metal frame, typically 42U tall (one "U" = 1.75 inches). Each server (a flat, pizza-box-shaped computer) occupies 1U or 2U of space. So a single rack holds roughly 20-42 physical servers. Each server has its own CPU, RAM, storage, and network ports.

What is a Top-of-Rack (ToR) Switch?

Now imagine 42 servers in a rack. They all need to talk to each other AND to servers in other racks. You can't run 42 separate cables from each server to a central switch across the room—that's a cable nightmare. Instead, you place a network switch at the top of each rack. This switch is called the Top-of-Rack (ToR) Switch.

How It Works
  • Every server in the rack plugs a short cable (1-2 meters) upward into the ToR switch.
  • The ToR switch then connects upward via high-speed fiber cables to an Aggregation Switch (also called a distribution switch), which connects multiple racks together.
  • The aggregation switches connect upward to Core Switches, which connect the entire data center to the outside internet.

⬆ The 3-tier data center network: ToR → Aggregation → Core → Internet. This is called a leaf-spine or hierarchical topology.

Why ToR Matters

  • Cable Management: Short cables within a rack, long fiber only between racks. Reduces physical chaos.
  • Performance: Servers in the same rack communicate at wire speed (10-100 Gbps) through the ToR switch—much faster than crossing the data center.
  • Fault Isolation: If a ToR switch dies, only its rack is affected. The rest of the data center keeps running.
  • Scalability: Need more capacity? Add a new rack with its own ToR switch and plug it into the aggregation layer.
Netflix's Scale Netflix doesn't own its own data centers for its core backend. It runs on Amazon Web Services (AWS). But Netflix does deploy its own hardware—Open Connect Appliances (OCAs)—inside ISP data centers worldwide for video delivery. Those OCAs sit in racks with ToR switches just like we described above.

3. Nexus Switches & MPPS

We just learned about ToR switches. But not all switches are created equal. In large-scale data centers like those Netflix runs on, you need industrial-grade networking equipment. Enter Cisco Nexus.

Beginner Analogy: Traffic Intersections A basic home switch is like a small T-junction in a village—handles a few cars. A Cisco Nexus switch is like a massive 16-lane highway interchange—handles millions of vehicles (data packets) per second without any traffic jams. It's built specifically for the insane traffic volumes inside data centers.
Deep Dive: The Internal Physics

What is a Nexus Switch?

Cisco Nexus is a family of data center switches designed for extreme performance, low latency, and programmability. Key features:

  • High Port Density: A single Nexus 9000 switch can have 36-128 ports running at 40/100 Gbps each.
  • Low Latency: Measured in microseconds (millionths of a second). When Netflix's recommendation engine queries a database, the response must travel through switches in under 5 microseconds.
  • Programmable (NX-OS): Runs a Linux-based operating system called NX-OS that engineers can automate and script—critical when you're managing thousands of switches.
  • VXLAN Support: Can create virtual network overlays (more on this in the SDN section).

What is MPPS (Million Packets Per Second)?

MPPS is the standard unit for measuring how fast a switch can process network packets. One packet is a small chunk of data (typically 64-1500 bytes). When Netflix streams video to 260 million users, the switches inside AWS's data centers are processing billions of packets per second.

Switch Type MPPS Rating Use Case
Home Router (TP-Link) ~1-5 MPPS Your house, 10 devices
Office Switch ~50-100 MPPS Small business, 200 employees
Cisco Nexus 9300 ~1,400+ MPPS Cloud data centers (AWS, Azure)
Cisco Nexus 9500 ~10,000+ MPPS Core backbone of massive DCs
Why MPPS Matters at Netflix Scale Netflix generates roughly 1 petabyte of network traffic per second during peak hours. Each petabyte consists of billions of individual packets. If your switches can't handle that MPPS rate, packets get dropped—users see buffering, stuttering, or errors. Nexus switches are specifically engineered to ensure zero packet loss at these rates.

4. Virtualization & The Logic Behind Dividing VMs

We now have racks of powerful physical servers connected by high-speed switches. But here's the problem: if Netflix ran one application per physical server, it would waste 85% of each server's capacity. The solution: virtualization—splitting one physical server into many virtual ones.

Beginner Analogy: The Apartment Building (Revisited) You own a huge building (physical server). Instead of one family using the whole building, you divide it into apartments (Virtual Machines). Each family (application) gets their own door, bathroom, and kitchen. They share the building's foundation and plumbing (hardware + hypervisor), but they live in isolated units. The property manager (Hypervisor) decides how many apartments to carve out and how much space each one gets.
Deep Dive: The Internal Physics

The Logic Behind Dividing a Physical Machine into VMs

When AWS (which Netflix runs on) receives a physical server—let's say it has 128 CPU cores, 512 GB RAM, and 10 TB storage—it doesn't just randomly chop it up. There's a precise engineering logic:

Step 1: Resource Inventory

The hypervisor (AWS uses a custom version of KVM called Nitro) first catalogs all available resources on the physical host:

Total CPU: 128 cores
Total RAM: 512 GB
Total Storage: 10 TB NVMe SSD
Total Network: 100 Gbps
Reserved for Hypervisor: 2 cores, 4 GB RAM (the "tax" for management)

Step 2: Define Instance Types (T-shirt Sizes)

AWS pre-defines "instance types" like t-shirt sizes. Each type allocates a fixed slice:

Instance Type vCPUs RAM Use Case at Netflix
t3.micro 2 1 GB Internal tools, monitoring agents
m5.xlarge 4 16 GB API servers, microservices
r5.4xlarge 16 128 GB In-memory caching (EVCache)
p3.8xlarge 32 244 GB + 4 GPUs ML model training for recommendations

Step 3: Bin-Packing Algorithm

AWS uses a bin-packing algorithm to fit as many VMs as possible onto each physical server without exceeding its capacity. Think of it like playing Tetris—you're trying to fill the physical server with VMs of different sizes, leaving minimal wasted space.

# Simplified bin-packing logic:
Physical Host = 126 available cores, 508 GB RAM

Place 1x r5.4xlarge  (16 cores, 128 GB)  → 110 cores, 380 GB left
Place 2x m5.xlarge   (8 cores, 32 GB)    → 102 cores, 348 GB left
Place 10x t3.micro   (20 cores, 10 GB)   → 82 cores, 338 GB left
... and so on until the host is full.

Step 4: Isolation Enforcement

Once VMs are placed, the hypervisor ensures total isolation:

  • CPU Pinning: Each VM's virtual CPUs are "pinned" to specific physical cores so they don't interfere with each other.
  • Memory Isolation: Hardware features (Intel VT-x / AMD-V) create memory fences—VM-1 physically cannot read VM-2's RAM.
  • I/O Isolation: Network and disk traffic are separated using SR-IOV (Single Root I/O Virtualization), which gives each VM a dedicated virtual network card at near-hardware speed.

5. Placement Groups

You now know that VMs run on physical servers scattered across a data center. But where your VMs land on the physical hardware matters enormously for performance and reliability.

Beginner Analogy: Seating Arrangement at a Wedding You're planning a wedding dinner. You have 200 guests and 20 tables. You don't seat people randomly. You put close friends at the same table (so they chat fast—low latency). You put rival families at different tables (so if one table catches fire, the other family survives—fault tolerance). Placement Groups are the same concept for VMs.
Deep Dive: The Internal Physics

Three Types of Placement Groups

1. Cluster Placement Group (Same Table)

All VMs on the same physical rack / nearby racks Use case: Netflix's real-time data pipeline. When the recommendation engine needs to query a cache server, that round-trip must take under 0.1 milliseconds. By placing both VMs in a Cluster Placement Group, they sit on the same physical rack and communicate through the high-speed ToR switch instead of crossing the data center.

Trade-off: If that rack loses power, ALL your VMs go down together.

2. Spread Placement Group (Different Tables)

Each VM on a different physical host Use case: Netflix's critical control-plane services (like the API gateway that receives every single user request). You NEVER want all copies of this service on the same physical machine. A Spread Group guarantees each VM runs on separate hardware—if one machine dies, the others survive.

Limit: Maximum 7 instances per Availability Zone per group.

3. Partition Placement Group (Different Table Sections)

VMs spread across logical partitions (each partition = different rack) Use case: Netflix's Cassandra database clusters. Cassandra stores copies of your viewing history across multiple nodes. By using Partition Placement, each copy lands on a different rack. If an entire rack fails, Cassandra still has copies on other racks and serves your data without interruption.

6. SDN: Software-Defined Networking

In the old world, every network switch and router had its own brain (firmware). If you wanted to change how traffic flows—say, redirect all Netflix video traffic through a faster path—an engineer had to log into each switch individually and type commands. In a data center with 10,000 switches, this is impossible. SDN solved this by separating the "brain" from the "body."

Beginner Analogy: Air Traffic Control In the old days, imagine each airplane pilot decided their own route. Chaos. Planes would collide. Then we invented Air Traffic Control (ATC)—a central tower that tells every plane exactly where to fly. SDN is the ATC for network traffic. Instead of each switch deciding where to send packets, a central SDN Controller tells all switches what to do.
Deep Dive: The Internal Physics

The Two Planes of Networking

Every network device does two jobs:

  1. Control Plane (The Brain): Decides WHERE to send traffic. "If a packet is going to 10.0.1.50, send it out port 3." This is the routing logic.
  2. Data Plane (The Muscle): Actually MOVES the packets at wire speed based on the brain's instructions.

In traditional networking, both planes live inside each switch. In SDN, you rip out the brain from every switch and put it in one central server called the SDN Controller. The switches become dumb packet-forwarding machines that just follow orders.

⬆ Traditional: each switch has its own brain. SDN: one central brain controls all switches.

Why Netflix Needs SDN

  • Dynamic Traffic Engineering: When "Squid Game Season 2" drops and traffic in Asia spikes 10x, SDN can instantly re-route traffic through less congested paths—no human intervention.
  • Multi-Tenancy: AWS runs Netflix, Uber, Airbnb, and thousands of others on the same hardware. SDN creates isolated virtual networks for each customer so their traffic never mixes.
  • Automation: Deploying 1,000 new VMs with specific networking rules takes seconds with SDN. Manually? Weeks.
  • Programmability: SDN controllers expose APIs. Netflix engineers can write code that says "if latency to region X > 50ms, failover to region Y"—the network responds automatically.
Key Protocol: OpenFlow The standard protocol for SDN controllers to talk to switches is called OpenFlow. It defines a set of rules (called flow tables) that the controller pushes to each switch. Each rule says: "If a packet matches [condition], do [action]." For example: "If destination IP is 10.0.5.0/24, forward to port 7."

7. OVS: Open vSwitch

We said SDN separates the brain from the body. But what is the "body" when your servers are virtual? Physical switches connect physical servers. What connects virtual machines to each other inside the same physical host? That's where Open vSwitch (OVS) comes in.

Beginner Analogy: The Internal Telephony System Imagine a big office building. There's a physical switchboard in the basement that connects the building to the outside phone network. But inside the building, each floor has its own internal phone system so employees on the same floor can call each other without going through the building's main switchboard. OVS is that internal phone system—it's a software-based switch running inside each physical server, connecting all the VMs on that server to each other and to the outside network.
Deep Dive: The Internal Physics

What OVS Actually Does

Open vSwitch is a production-quality, open-source virtual switch that runs as software inside the hypervisor (the host operating system). It does everything a physical switch does, but in software:

  • L2 Switching: Forwards Ethernet frames between VMs on the same host based on MAC addresses—no physical cable needed.
  • VLAN Tagging: Can tag VM traffic with VLAN IDs to isolate networks (VM-1's traffic stays separate from VM-2's traffic even though they share the same physical NIC).
  • Tunneling (VXLAN/GRE): Can wrap VM traffic in a tunnel to send it across the physical network to VMs on other hosts. This is how a VM on Host-A talks to a VM on Host-B as if they're on the same local network.
  • Flow Tables: Just like a physical SDN switch, OVS has flow tables programmed by an SDN controller. Each rule says what to do with matching packets.
  • QoS (Quality of Service): Can prioritize certain traffic—for example, Netflix can ensure that video streaming packets get priority over logging packets.

Performance: Kernel vs. DPDK By default, OVS runs in the Linux kernel, processing packets at ~1-5 million packets/second. For data-center-scale workloads, OVS can use DPDK (Data Plane Development Kit)—a technology that bypasses the kernel entirely and processes packets in user-space, reaching 20-40+ million packets/second. AWS uses a similar approach with their custom Nitro cards that offload networking to dedicated hardware.

8. OVN: Open Virtual Network

OVS is powerful but it's just a switch—it only knows how to forward packets based on rules someone else gives it. Who writes those rules? Who coordinates all the OVS instances across thousands of physical servers? That's OVN (Open Virtual Network).

Beginner Analogy: The Government vs. Local Police OVS is like a local police station—it enforces laws (forwarding rules) in its area (physical server). But someone has to write the laws and make sure every police station follows the same set of laws. OVN is the central government that writes the networking laws and distributes them to all OVS instances across the data center.
Deep Dive: The Internal Physics

OVN Architecture

OVN is the SDN controller specifically designed to work with OVS. It has two main components:

1. OVN Northbound Database (NB DB)

This stores the desired network state in logical terms that humans can understand: "Create a virtual switch. Attach VM-1 and VM-2 to it. Allow traffic between them on port 80." Cloud platforms like OpenStack or Kubernetes talk to this database.

2. OVN Southbound Database (SB DB)

This translates the logical state into physical flow rules that OVS understands: "On Host-12, if a packet with MAC AA:BB:CC has destination IP 10.0.1.5, wrap it in a VXLAN tunnel with VNI 1001 and send it to Host-37 via the physical NIC." These rules are pushed down to every OVS instance.

⬆ OVN translates human-readable network intent into machine-readable flow rules pushed to every OVS instance.

What OVN Provides on Top of OVS

  • Logical Switches: Virtual L2 network spanning multiple physical hosts. VMs on different servers talk as if on the same LAN.
  • Logical Routers: Virtual L3 routing between different logical switches. This is how Netflix's API server subnet (10.0.1.0/24) talks to the database subnet (10.0.2.0/24).
  • ACLs (Access Control Lists): Security rules—"VM-1 can talk to VM-2 on port 443, but VM-3 cannot." This implements Security Groups (covered next).
  • Load Balancing: Built-in L4 load balancing—distribute traffic across multiple VMs.
  • NAT/SNAT: Network Address Translation so VMs with private IPs can reach the internet through a shared public IP.

9. VPC & CIDR: Your Private Cloud Kingdom

All the technologies we've covered so far—SDN, OVS, OVN—come together to create one of the most important cloud concepts: the VPC (Virtual Private Cloud). This is what Netflix actually uses day-to-day on AWS.

Beginner Analogy: A Gated Community Imagine you buy land in a city (AWS). You build a gated community (VPC) with its own internal roads, its own address numbering system (street 1, house 1 through house 254), and its own security guards at the gate. People inside your community can freely visit each other. But outsiders can't get in unless you specifically allow them. The VPC is your private, isolated section of the public cloud.
Deep Dive: The Internal Physics

What is a VPC?

A VPC is a logically isolated virtual network within a cloud provider (AWS, Azure, GCP). When Netflix creates a VPC on AWS, they get:

  • Their own private IP address space (no other AWS customer uses the same IPs inside Netflix's VPC).
  • Complete network isolation—powered by OVN/OVS and VXLAN tunnels underneath.
  • Full control over subnets, route tables, gateways, and security rules.

What is CIDR? (Classless Inter-Domain Routing)

When you create a VPC, you must define its address range using CIDR notation. CIDR is just a compact way to say "I want X number of IP addresses."

Understanding CIDR Notation 10.0.0.0/16 — Let's break this down:
  • 10.0.0.0 is the starting IP address.
  • /16 means "the first 16 bits of the address are FIXED (the network part). The remaining 16 bits are FREE for individual hosts."
  • 16 free bits = 2^16 = 65,536 possible IP addresses (from 10.0.0.0 to 10.0.255.255).
CIDR Block Free Bits Available IPs Analogy
10.0.0.0/8 24 bits 16,777,216 An entire country's address system
10.0.0.0/16 16 bits 65,536 A large city's address system
10.0.1.0/24 8 bits 256 One neighborhood's address system
10.0.1.0/28 4 bits 16 One apartment building's unit numbers

How Netflix Designs Its VPC

Netflix doesn't throw everything into one big network. They create subnets (sub-networks) within the VPC to organize services:

Netflix VPC: 10.0.0.0/16 (65,536 IPs total)

├── Public Subnet:  10.0.1.0/24 (256 IPs) → Load Balancers, API Gateways
│   (Internet-facing. Has a route to the Internet Gateway.)
│
├── Private Subnet: 10.0.10.0/24 (256 IPs) → Microservices (Recommendation, Auth)
│   (No direct internet. Talks to public subnet through internal routing.)
│
├── Private Subnet: 10.0.20.0/24 (256 IPs) → Databases (Cassandra, EVCache)
│   (Even more locked down. Only microservices can reach these.)
│
└── Private Subnet: 10.0.30.0/24 (256 IPs) → ML Training Instances (GPU)
    (Isolated. Only data pipelines feed into these.)

10. Security Groups: The Bouncer at Every Door

Subnets divide your VPC into neighborhoods, but you also need security at the individual VM level. That's what Security Groups do—they are virtual firewalls attached to each VM.

Beginner Analogy: The Nightclub Bouncer Imagine each VM is a room in a nightclub. The Security Group is the bouncer standing at the door of that room. The bouncer has a guest list (rules). If your name (IP address) is on the list AND you're going to the right room (port), you get in. Otherwise, you're denied entry. By default, the bouncer blocks everyone—you have to explicitly add names to the guest list.
Deep Dive: The Internal Physics

How Security Groups Work

  • Stateful: If you allow inbound traffic on port 80, the response traffic is automatically allowed out. You don't need a separate outbound rule for the response.
  • Allow-only: You can only write "ALLOW" rules. There are no "DENY" rules. If something isn't explicitly allowed, it's implicitly denied.
  • Applied per-instance: Each VM/instance can have one or more Security Groups. The rules are evaluated as a union (if ANY group allows the traffic, it goes through).
  • Source can be another SG: Instead of specifying an IP, you can say "Allow traffic from any instance in Security Group X." This is how Netflix allows its API servers to talk to databases without hardcoding IPs.

Netflix Example: Security Group Configuration

# Security Group: "netflix-api-sg" (attached to API servers)
Inbound Rules:
  - Allow TCP port 443 from 0.0.0.0/0     (HTTPS from the internet)
  - Allow TCP port 22 from 10.0.100.0/24  (SSH only from admin subnet)
Outbound Rules:
  - Allow ALL traffic to 0.0.0.0/0         (API servers can reach anything)

# Security Group: "netflix-db-sg" (attached to Cassandra databases)
Inbound Rules:
  - Allow TCP port 9042 from "netflix-api-sg"  (Only API servers can query DB)
  - Allow TCP port 7000 from "netflix-db-sg"   (DB nodes can replicate to each other)
Outbound Rules:
  - Allow ALL traffic to 0.0.0.0/0
How It's Implemented Under the Hood Security Group rules are ultimately translated into OVN ACLs (Access Control Lists), which get pushed as flow rules into each OVS instance. When a packet arrives at OVS on the host, it checks the flow table: "Does this packet match an ALLOW rule? No? Drop it." This happens at line rate—millions of times per second—with zero noticeable latency.

11. Route Tables & Internal Routing

We have subnets inside a VPC. But how does traffic flow between subnets? The answer: Route Tables—the road signs of your network.

Beginner Analogy: Road Signs Road signs at every intersection tell you: "Airport → Right. Downtown → Straight." Route tables tell packets: "To reach this destination → go through that gateway."
Deep Dive: The Internal Physics

What a Route Table Contains

10.0.0.0/16        local               "VPC traffic → stay internal"
0.0.0.0/0          nat-gw-xyz789       "Internet-bound → NAT Gateway"

How Subnets Talk to Each Other

When API server (10.0.1.50) queries Cassandra (10.0.20.100):

  1. OS checks: "Is 10.0.20.100 in my subnet? No."
  2. Sends to default gateway (VPC router).
  3. Router checks: "10.0.20.100 matches 10.0.0.0/16 → local."
  4. Forwards internally—never touches the internet.
  5. OVS/OVN delivers via VXLAN tunnels.
NAT Gateway: The Translator Private subnets have no public IP. The NAT Gateway replaces the private source IP with its own public IP for outbound traffic, then routes responses back. This is how Netflix's internal services reach the internet without direct exposure.

17. Dynamic Scaling: Growing & Shrinking on Demand

Netflix traffic follows predictable patterns: low at 6 AM, massive at 9 PM. Running peak-capacity servers 24/7 wastes money. Auto-scaling adds servers during peaks and removes them during lulls.

Beginner Analogy: Uber Surge Uber doesn't keep every driver on the road 24/7. During rain, surge pricing activates more drivers. At 3 AM, most go home. Auto-scaling does this with servers—spin up during "surge" and terminate when quiet.
Deep Dive: The Internal Physics

Two Types of Scaling

TypeWhat It DoesNetflix Example
HorizontalAdd MORE serversSpin up 50 more API instances during "Wednesday" premiere
VerticalMake servers BIGGERUpgrade ML training instance from 16 to 64 vCPUs

How Netflix Auto-Scales

Netflix uses Titus (container platform) + AWS Auto Scaling Groups:

  1. Metrics Collection: Every service reports CPU, memory, request latency, and queue depth to Atlas (Netflix's monitoring system) every 10 seconds.
  2. Scaling Policy: "If average CPU across the Recommendation cluster exceeds 70% for 5 minutes → add 10 instances."
  3. Launch: Titus spins up 10 new containers (takes ~30 seconds since containers are lightweight).
  4. Registration: New containers register with Eureka (service discovery). Zuul starts routing traffic to them.
  5. Scale Down: When CPU drops below 30% for 15 minutes → terminate excess containers (with connection draining).
Predictive Scaling Netflix doesn't just react—it predicts. Using historical data, they know that traffic in the US peaks at 9 PM EST every day. So scaling begins at 8:30 PM, before the spike hits. This eliminates the 2-3 minute delay of reactive scaling.

Circuit Breaker Pattern (Hystrix)

If the Recommendation service is slow, it could block Zuul and take down ALL services. Hystrix prevents this:

  1. Zuul calls Rec Engine with a timeout (e.g., 500ms).
  2. If 50% of calls fail within 10 seconds → Hystrix opens the circuit.
  3. All subsequent calls immediately return a fallback response (e.g., generic "trending" recommendations instead of personalized ones).
  4. After 30 seconds, Hystrix sends a test request. If successful → close the circuit and resume normal operation.
Why Microservices Fail Without These Patterns Without circuit breakers, a single slow database query can cascade: DB slow → Rec Engine waits → Zuul threads exhausted → Auth Service can't respond → entire Netflix goes down. Hystrix ensures failures stay isolated.

Key Security Components

  • Password Hashing (bcrypt): Netflix never stores your actual password. It's hashed with bcrypt (computationally expensive, resistant to brute force). Even if the DB is breached, attackers get useless hashes.
  • JWT (JSON Web Token): A self-contained token that carries the user's identity + permissions. The key innovation: no database call needed to validate it. The gateway just checks the mathematical signature. This saves millions of DB queries per day.
  • Refresh Tokens: JWTs expire quickly (e.g., 15 minutes). Refresh tokens last longer (weeks) and are used to get new JWTs without re-entering the password.
  • Device Fingerprinting: Netflix tracks which devices are logged in. "5 screens max" is enforced by counting active device tokens.
  • Regional DRM (Widevine/FairPlay): Even after authentication, video content is encrypted with DRM. The decryption key is tied to your geographic location and subscription tier.

21. Open Connect: Netflix's Custom CDN

Netflix doesn't stream video from AWS. That would be too slow and too expensive. Instead, they built their own Content Delivery Network called Open Connect—one of the largest CDNs in the world.

Beginner Analogy: Local Water Tanks Imagine a water company. They don't pump water from the main reservoir (AWS) every time someone turns on a tap. Instead, they pre-fill local water tanks in every neighborhood. When you turn on your tap, water comes from the nearby tank—fast and cheap. Open Connect places Netflix servers inside your ISP's building, pre-loaded with popular content.
Deep Dive: The Internal Physics

How Open Connect Works

  1. OCA Deployment: Netflix gives ISPs free hardware boxes called Open Connect Appliances (OCAs). These are custom-built servers with 100+ TB of SSD storage.
  2. Content Pre-positioning: During off-peak hours (2-6 AM), Netflix pushes the most popular content to OCAs worldwide. "Wednesday" premiering Friday? By Thursday night, it's already cached on OCAs in every major ISP.
  3. Steering: When you press play, Netflix's backend (on AWS) decides which OCA is closest to you and redirects your request there. Your video streams from a server literally inside your ISP's building—not from across the ocean.
  4. Fallback: If the local OCA doesn't have the content (rare, niche title), it falls back to a regional OCA, then to the AWS origin.
